Nuenen

1883-1886

Nuenen was smaller than Etten, and in December 1883 it must have seemed unlikely that Van Gogh would spend almost two years here, in close proximity to his family. His parents put themselves out to understand and come to terms with their prodigal son, even repairing an old shed that had been used for laundering so that Vincent could have a private studio outside of parsonage. Relations remained uneasy and he was soon complaining to Theo that ‘the welcome home was in every respect kind and cordial, but at bottom there is not the least change in what i must call their blindness to, and their total lack of understanding of, the relations between us.

Evidently it never occurred to Van Gogh that a couple in their sixties were not likely to change their ideas radically, or that the onus of maintaining satisfactory relations might lie with him. In many ways he was at his most unreasonable in his dealing with family, blaming his father for corrupting Theo by encouraging his business career, and blaming Theo alternately for remaining a businessman and for not pushing Vincent’s paintings energetically enough. In calmer moments, he realized how much he owed Theo (and not just financially), and in February 1884 he began to pay Theo for his regular subsidies by sending him paintings. Soon he was sending all of his paintings to Paris, declaring that everything he created was in reality a joint effort, for which Theo deserved at least half the credit.

Paris

1886-1888

Vincent Van Gogh was one of the greatest pioneers of modern art. Almost everything forward-looking in painting and sculpture was nourished in Paris between the 1850’s and the early years of World War II. Not surprisingly, then, Van Gogh’s experience was one of the crucial events in his life: without it he would have been an entirely different sort of artist.

The impact of Paris on Van Gogh is the most evident in his canvases. Van Gogh was exposed to both Impressionism and Neo-Impressionism simultaneously, and their influences were felt, slowly but surely, in his painting. His actual movements are hard to trace, as we have few letters written by him: he was living with Theo, his principal correspondent, so there was no need to communicate with the pen. He had the company of his brother to assuage his loneliness and a great city’s picturesque views and a variety of life to entertain him.

Arles

1888-1889

Van Gogh left Paris on 20 February, 1888. At the end of a long journey by train to Arles, he discovered that the sunny south was covered in winter snow. Then, despite the unusually late fall of snow, blossoms began to appear on the trees and Van Gogh, like many artists before him, was overwhelmed with joy on first experiencing the light and color of the South.

Van Gogh himself wrote that he struck a ‘high yellow note’ in the summer of 1888. Although it would be rash to assert that Van Gogh had a ‘favorite’ color, he certainly saw yellow as the key to the South, instancing his hero Monticelli, who had painted it ‘all yellow, orange, and sulphur colors

Saint Remy & Auvers-sur-Oise

1890

The asylum of St-Paul-de-Mausole was situated a kilometre or so outside the town of St. Remy, in the countryside no doubt chosen to afford maximum peace and quiet for the occupants. By 1889, however, the place seems to have fallen on evil days. The entire asylum had a run-down air. Accustomed to an austere way of life, he seemed untroubled by the gloom of the place and even took some comfort from the knowledge he was not alone in suffering from hallucinations.
